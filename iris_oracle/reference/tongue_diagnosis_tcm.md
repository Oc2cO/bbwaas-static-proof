# Tongue Diagnosis in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

## Overview

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the tongue is considered a mirror of the body’s internal state.  Practitioners examine the tongue’s colour, coating, shape and moisture to identify patterns of qi, blood, yin and yang【46909610175728†L331-L405】.  Each part of the tongue corresponds to specific organs: the **tip** reflects the heart and lungs, the **centre** corresponds to the stomach and spleen, the **sides** relate to the liver and gallbladder, and the **back** (root) connects to the kidneys, intestines and bladder【46909610175728†L415-L429】.  Tongue diagnosis is a tool for holistic insight rather than medical diagnosis.

## Observation Categories

### 1. Colour

- **Pale:**  Indicates deficiency of qi or blood; can suggest fatigue or cold【46909610175728†L331-L405】.【173319014746590†L100-L231】
- **Healthy pink:**  Signifies balanced energy and good circulation【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
- **Red/dark red:**  Suggests heat or inflammation in the body【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
- **Purple/blue:**  Associated with blood stasis or poor circulation; may reflect chronic pain or stress【173319014746590†L100-L231】.

### 2. Coating

- **Thin white coating:**  Considered normal and healthy【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
- **Thick white coating:**  May indicate cold or dampness, sluggish digestion or phlegm【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
- **Thick yellow coating:**  Linked to heat or infection【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
- **No coating (peeled or cracked):**  Suggests deficiency of yin or vital fluids; associated with dehydration or long‑term stress【46909610175728†L331-L405】.

### 3. Shape and Size

- **Swollen/puffy with scalloped edges:**  Implies dampness, fluid retention or spleen qi deficiency【46909610175728†L413-L456】.  The scallops (tooth marks) are thought to show that the tongue presses against the teeth due to swelling.
- **Thin:**  Indicates dehydration or blood/yin deficiency【46909610175728†L413-L456】.
- **Cracks:**  May denote chronic conditions or constitutional weaknesses; the position of cracks can correlate with specific organs【173319014746590†L100-L231】.

### 4. Moisture

- **Moderately moist:**  Represents healthy fluid balance.
- **Too wet:**  Signals cold or damp accumulation【173319014746590†L100-L231】.
- **Dry:**  Indicates heat, dehydration or yin deficiency【173319014746590†L100-L231】.

## How to Observe the Tongue

1. **Timing:**  View the tongue in natural light first thing in the morning before eating, drinking or brushing teeth【46909610175728†L346-L349】.
2. **Lighting:**  Use soft daylight; artificial light can distort colours.
3. **Position:**  Ask the individual to stick out the tongue gently (not strained) so that the entire surface can be seen.
4. **Note regions:**  Map observations to the corresponding organ regions: tip (heart/lungs), centre (stomach/spleen), sides (liver/gallbladder), back (kidneys/intestines/bladder)【46909610175728†L415-L429】.
5. **Holistic interpretation:**  Combine colour, coating, shape and moisture to look for patterns rather than isolated signs.  The findings should prompt questions and self‑care rather than definitive diagnoses.

## Reading Template

Each tongue reading uses a four‑section structure:

1. **What the Oracle Sees** – Describe the tongue’s colour, coating, shape and moisture.  Note specific regions (tip, centre, sides, back) and any cracks or scalloped edges.
2. **What This Reveals** – Interpret these features according to TCM principles: e.g., pale tongue may point to qi deficiency, a thick yellow coating may hint at heat or digestive infection, and a swollen tongue with scallops may suggest dampness【46909610175728†L331-L405】.
3. **A Whisper** – Offer gentle, non‑medical guidance such as dietary suggestions (warm foods, hydration) or mindfulness practices.  Emphasise balance and listening to the body.
4. **Look Ahead** – Provide a hopeful observation about how awareness and lifestyle adjustments may support vitality in the future.
